# Mercy Ethos and Style Guide

This document summarizes Mercy's organizational identity, values, and communication style. Reference this when creating content for Mercy caregivers.

---

## Mission Statement

> "As the Sisters of Mercy before us, we bring to life the healing ministry of Jesus through our compassionate care and exceptional service."

---

## Core Values

Mercy's five foundational values (with **Dignity** as the foundation for all others):

| Value | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Dignity** | Recognizing each person's inherent worth as created in God's image |
| **Excellence** | Providing the highest standard of care |
| **Justice** | Maintaining right relationships, with focus on economically disadvantaged populations |
| **Service** | Prioritizing others' needs |
| **Stewardship** | Wisely managing resources to strengthen the ministry |

## Charism (Grace-Based Gifts)

Five characteristics define Mercy's spiritual identity:

1. **Bias for action** — Moving forward rather than waiting
2. **Entrepreneurial courage** — Willingness to innovate and take risks
3. **Hospitality** — Welcoming all who seek care
4. **Right relationships** — Built on honest communication
5. **Fullness of life** — Maintaining joy even during suffering

## Heritage: Catherine McAuley and the Sisters of Mercy

- **Founded:** Catherine McAuley established the Sisters of Mercy in Dublin, Ireland in 1827
- **Catalyst:** McAuley "woke up in the middle of the night, weeping" from dreams of destitute women and children seeking help
- **Innovation:** One of the first religious orders whose Sisters were free to walk among the poor and visit them in their homes
- **U.S. Arrival:** Sisters arrived in America in 1843, St. Louis by 1856
- **First Hospital:** 25-bed infirmary for women and children, 15 years after St. Louis arrival
- **Modern Mercy:** Individual hospitals consolidated in 1986 to form the Sisters of Mercy Health System

## Communication Style Guidelines

### Tone
- **Compassionate** — Acknowledge concerns, lead with empathy
- **Supportive** — Caregivers are valued partners, not interchangeable resources
- **Honest** — Direct communication, even about difficult topics
- **Hopeful** — Joy and purpose even in challenging work
- **Respectful** — Honor the dignity of every person

### Language
- Use "caregivers" (not employees)
- Use "caregivers" broadly (all roles contribute to patient care)
- Reference heritage when connecting to mission ("As Catherine McAuley taught us...")
- Connect innovations to values ("...because every person deserves dignity")
- Avoid jargon; Grade 6-8 reading level for broad audience

### Visual and Brand Elements
- Mercy brand colors (refer to official brand guidelines)
- Healthcare-specific icons
- Imagery that reflects diverse communities served
- Human connection emphasized over technology

## Key Themes for AI Training Content

When discussing AI at Mercy, connect to these themes:

1. **Human dignity preserved** — AI supports caregivers; it doesn't replace human connection
2. **Compassionate care first** — Technology serves the mission, not the other way around
3. **Stewardship** — Using AI responsibly protects patients and resources
4. **Justice** — AI must serve all patients equitably, without bias
5. **Excellence** — AI helps deliver the highest standard of care
6. **Catherine McAuley's legacy** — Serving the vulnerable with innovation and courage
